 Cross River Gorilla | Behaviour | Habitat | Threats  

Habitat Loss

Deforestation

Once hunted to near extinction by locals, habitat loss is now the looming threat to the survival of Cross River gorillas. Lush forests have been razed and transformed for agriculture, cattle grazing, and the creation of logging roads.

Human Expansion

Many small human-settlements, that continue to expand, are scattered throughout the gorillas’ range, further fragmenting the forest and making it impossible for gorillas to migrate between their mountain strongholds. Upgraded roads in the gorillas’ range also contribute to habitat loss and fragmentation, as they open up new areas for settlement and cultivation, and act as possible barriers to migration.

Hunting

As forests are opened up by timber companies, impoverished hunters move in seeking monetary gain from these apes. 

Despite the fact that hunting gorillas is illegal in Cameroon and Nigeria, the gorillas remain vulnerable to poaching for bush meat as well as ape smuggling for financial gain.

Loss of Genetic Diversity

The tiny remaining population of the Cross River Gorilla means that genetic diversity is low and vulnerable to inbreeding, which can make successful reproduction more difficult.

Although areas of unoccupied potential gorilla habitat still remain and can provide connectivity between the subpopulations, these areas are not yet safe for gorillas to use.

Human Diseases

Inbreeding and the transmission of human diseases, specifically the Ebola virus, also threaten this great ape’s survival because their low genetic diversity leaves them less able to adapt.

Even if the rate of ebola mortality along with hunting was rebated, the promise of these gorillas making a fast recovery is virtually non-existent.[vague] The reproduction rate of the Cross River gorilla is low and is estimated to take 75 years to fully recover the population.
